Executive Summary
The structural gap between what education delivers and what the market demands

Formal education systems — universities, professional programmes, vocational institutions — operate on an implicit promise: invest years in structured learning, and emerge with skills the labour market values. This framework interrogates that promise. It finds not that the promise is false, but that it is structurally time-limited in ways that neither institutions nor graduates typically account for.

Two mechanisms compress the value of education before it is even applied. First, there is curriculum lag: the inevitable delay between the moment a skill becomes market-relevant and the moment a university incorporates it into a degree programme. Second, there is study duration: the years spent completing a programme during which market conditions continue to evolve. Together, these constitute the lag (L) — and by the time a graduate crosses a threshold into employment, a meaningful portion of their training is already obsolescent.

This is the subject of the first model, Version 1. It identifies and quantifies the structural compression of employability — the shrinking of what we call the Effective Employability Window (EEW).

Version 2 extends the analysis into what happens after workforce entry. Not all skills decay at the same rate — in fact, some do not decay at all. Under the right conditions, certain skills compound: they generate returns that increase with use, experience, and time. The key insight of Version 2 is that the distinction between compounding and decaying skills is not random — it can be predicted, designed for, and strategically optimised.

Central finding

"The value of a formal education is eroded twice: once before it is applied, through structural lag; and once after, through skill decay — unless the graduate has acquired skills capable of compounding."

Section 01 — Version 1
The pre-entry problem: lag, compression, and the relevance gap

The first model establishes the foundational mechanics. It introduces three temporal events that define the employability timeline: the moment a market skill need emerges, the moment an educational institution adopts that skill into its curriculum, and the moment a student graduates and enters the workforce.

The gap between emergence and curriculum adoption is Component A — institutional lag. Universities operate on governance cycles; syllabi are approved annually or less frequently; faculty hiring and retraining take time. A skill that becomes economically salient in year zero may not appear in an accredited programme until year two or three.

The gap between curriculum adoption and graduation is Component B — study duration. A three- or four-year degree means that even skills added to a curriculum in the student's first year are, by graduation, three years old. In slower-moving fields this is manageable. In fast-moving ones — technology, finance, data science, applied AI — it is catastrophic.

Together, A plus B equals L, the total lag. The Effective Employability Window — the period during which the graduate's skills are genuinely relevant — is what remains of the skill's market lifespan after that lag is deducted.

The practical implications of Version 1 are threefold. First, graduates are structurally disadvantaged at the moment of entry — not through any personal failure, but through an architectural feature of formal education systems. Second, this disadvantage is asymmetric across industries: in rapidly-evolving fields, the compression is acute; in stable professions, it is manageable. Third, the natural response — continuous and real-time learning — shifts economic value away from credentials and toward adaptive capacity.

"The value of education is not only eroded over time — it is already diminished before it is applied."

Section 02 — Version 2
The post-entry question: which skills compound, and which decay?

Version 1 answers the question of where you start. It shows that every graduate enters the labour market with a structural deficit — a relevance gap that is not their fault, but is nonetheless real. However, Version 1 treats all skills as equivalent after graduation: all subject to the same linear decay. This is empirically false and strategically misleading.

Version 2 introduces a third force: compounding (κ). Compounding describes the capacity of certain skills to appreciate with use — to generate not diminishing but accelerating returns as they are practised, transferred, and applied across contexts. Opposed to it is decay (δ), which characterises skills tied to specific tools, platforms, or contextual configurations that become obsolete as environments change.

The visual representation splits the single post-graduation trajectory of Version 1 into three distinct paths. The compounding path rises steeply — these are the skills that make a senior professional disproportionately valuable. The decay path falls toward irrelevance — these are skills that must be continuously replenished just to remain employable. And the hybrid arc, which characterises most careers, shows an initial dip followed by recovery: the graduate devalues slightly as their specific training ages, then compounds as they develop genuine expertise and transferable judgement.

Abstract and transferable skills

The most realistic career trajectory. A slight relevance dip immediately post-graduation as specific training ages, followed by compounding returns as the professional builds genuine, cross-contextual expertise and leverage. Designing for this arc — rather than either extreme — is the practical objective of career strategy.

The critical strategic question is therefore not merely how much a skill decays, but whether it will compound. Four structural properties predict this with reasonable reliability.

Transferability
Applies across domains, roles, and industries. Not locked to a specific institutional, technological, or sectoral context. Generates value when moved.
Abstraction level
Higher-order principles outlast implementations. Frameworks outlast syntax. The ability to reason about a class of problems outlasts mastery of any specific instance.
Feedback loops
Practised repeatedly with legible outcomes. The skill has a mechanism by which use generates improvement — it gets sharper, not merely exercised.
Leverage potential
Amplifies other skills or other people's output. Returns scale with use rather than plateauing. Creates disproportionate value relative to time invested.
Synthesis

Lag explains where you start. Decay and compounding determine where you end.

Together, the two versions of this model define a complete analytical frame for evaluating the economic return on education. Version 1 establishes that entry point is structurally disadvantaged and that this disadvantage varies predictably by industry velocity. Version 2 establishes that the post-entry trajectory is not passive — it is a function of skill architecture — and that the four determinants of compounding can be used to design learning strategies, curriculum priorities, and career investments accordingly.

The practical implication for individuals is to audit their portfolio along both dimensions: how much lag compression do they face at entry, and what proportion of their skills have the structural properties that enable compounding? The practical implication for institutions is more uncomfortable: a curriculum optimised for entry-point relevance but composed predominantly of tool-bound, context-specific content may produce graduates who begin adequately but plateau or decline within a decade.

The model does not argue against technical skill acquisition. It argues for a specific sequencing and composition: a foundation of high-abstraction, high-transferability skills that compound, onto which context-specific technical competencies are layered — and refreshed — throughout a career.

Methodological notes
All curves in this framework are illustrative. They represent structural tendencies derived from analytical reasoning rather than empirically fitted regressions. The exponential form V(t) = V₀·eκt and V(t) = V₀·e−δt is chosen for analytical tractability and qualitative accuracy; actual skill value functions are likely more complex, discontinuous, and context-dependent. The framework is designed as a strategic thinking tool, not a predictive model. Readers should calibrate the rate constants κ and δ to their own field's observed rates of skill obsolescence and compounding.
